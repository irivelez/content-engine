# **High Converting Offers**

# **Bottom Up Approach**

This framework offers flexibility to start with a straightforward “Base” offer, then scale to a “Base \+ Performance” model for greater earning potential and client impact.

---

### **Two Levers of Your Offer**

1. Base Cost – Covers your service costs \+ profit margin

   * *One-off projects*: E.g., $10K for a website build.  
   * *Hourly or flexible services*: E.g., $10/hour for ongoing customer service.  
   * *Contracted retainers*: E.g., $1.5K for producing 2 YouTube videos monthly.

2. Performance-Based Compensation – Adds a performance-based incentive for meeting specific goals

   * *Revenue percentage*: E.g., 20% of AdSense revenue from YouTube videos.  
   * *Deals closed*: E.g., 10% commission on new business closed.  
   * *Profit share from ads*: E.g., 20% of profit from Meta Ads.

### **Offer Structures**

* Base Only: SIMPLE OFFER – Set fee with clear deliverables.

* Base \+ Performance: PARTNER/ PREMIUM OFFER – Combines set fee with a results-based incentive. 

### **Steps to Calculate Your Base Offer**

1. Estimate Your Base Price

   * Calculate the total expense of delivering the service plus a profit margin.   
     Aim for a margin of around 50% to ensure profitability.

2. Break Down Costs

   * Include the actual cost per hour for your time (or your team’s time) on client work, meetings and deliverables. Add any additional costs such as software, office expenses, or administrative fees.

   * Formula:   
     I **Hourly Rate × Hours for Project \+ Additional Costs \= Total Cost I** 

3. Example Calculation

   * If you charge $50/hour, expect a project to take 40 hours, and incur $500 in software costs:

     Cost: $50 × 40 \+ $500 \= $2,500  
     With Margin: $2,500 \+ 50% margin \= $3,750 Base Price

4. Consider Your Target Market

   * Smaller target markets (B2C): Lower base, less complexity (e.g., $3K for email marketing for a new clothing brand).  
   * Larger target markets (B2B): Higher base, more complexity (e.g., $10K for email marketing for a brand like Nike).

### **Performance-Based Structure**

* Define Performance Metrics

  * Choose metrics directly linked to outcomes, not just leads. 

  * Target conversion metrics (e.g., closed sales, ROI from ad spend) to avoid misalignment on lead quality.

* Examples of Performance Structures

  * A client that we run Meta and Google ads for  
    They have a $20K monthly ad budget and want a 2x ROI

  * Standard offer we could have created: $5K/month retainer  
  * Instead, the offer we created: $3K/month base \+ 20% of ad profit

  * In month 1 we made $20k profit on ads for them.   
  * So we made $3k/m \+ $4k (20% of 20k) \= $7k total

  * In 3 months, we had scaled their ad account to $40k spend and made around $20k+/m for us  
  


### **Benefits of the Base \+ Performance Approach**

1. Incentivizes continuous quality since earnings align with client success.

2. Differentiates your agency from competitors by demonstrating confidence and a commitment to measurable outcomes.

3. Instead of promising a certain number of leads, anchor your offer on your unique skill or expertise that directly impacts results.

# **Top Down Approach**

Develop an offer that is well-positioned, competitively priced and aligned with current market demands.

### **Step-by-Step Guide**

1. **Look at the Market**  
* **Spot Trends**: Research what’s currently popular in your industry to see where there’s high demand and where new needs are popping up.

* **Check Competitor Pricing**: Find out what similar services cost, looking at both top competitors and average prices.

* **Find Service Gaps**: Identify areas where the demand is high but not many people offer the service. These gaps are where you can stand out.

2. **Set Profit Goals**  
* **Calculate Profit Margins**: Use your costs and competitor pricing to estimate how much profit you can make. This helps you set pricing that covers costs and leaves room for profit.

* **Compare Profit Benchmarks**: Set a profit goal that meets or beats industry standards by factoring in things like service delivery, marketing, and other costs.

3. **Consider Upsell Opportunities**  
* **Premium Pricing**: If there’s high demand for your service, consider offering it at a premium price, especially if it’s unique or high quality.

* **Add Extra Services**: Think of any additional services that could justify a higher price, like faster delivery, ongoing support, or extra features.

4. **Find Your “Grey Area”**  
* **Locate the Middle Ground**: Find a price range that’s between low-budget and premium. This “grey area” is where customers often see the best value.

* **Highlight Your Unique Selling Points**: Explain why clients should pick you over cheaper or more expensive options by focusing on the specific benefits you offer, like faster results, measurable outcomes, or better quality.

# **Key Principles**

1. **No Money-Back Guarantee**  
   * Money-back guarantees don’t suit the agency model. Instead, focus on demonstrating value and delivering strong, results-oriented service.

2. **Custom Offers Only**  
   * Avoid fixed packages. Build offers around the specific service or deliverables each client requests.

   * **Example**:  
     If a client wants 4 YouTube videos per month, don’t show them packages with different options (e.g., 3, 5, or 10 videos with edits).   
     Instead, provide an exact quote for **4 videos** based on your pricing formula, which includes your desired profit margin.

3. **Pricing Flexibility**  
   * Since each offer is custom, you can adjust pricing based on deliverables and negotiate where needed to maintain profitability.

4. **Profitable Offers**  
   * Structure offers so that all options remain profitable, including downsell and upsell opportunities.

   * **Downsells**: Offer a smaller package with fewer deliverables if a client’s budget is limited.  
   * **Upsells**: Add extra services or premium options if the client shows interest in expanding their scope.
